Shape

  • Characteristic outline of a plane figure or the surface configuration of a volumetric form
    • Depends on visual contrast
    • The primary means by which we recognize, identify, and categorize particular figures and forms
  • Shapes of:
    • Floor, wall, ceiling planes
    • Door and window openings
    • Silhouettes and contours of building forms



  • Circle: centralized, introverted, self-centering
  • Triangle: stability when resting on one side, or unstable when resting on one vertices
  • Square: pure, rational, like the triangle, stable on sides, unstable on vertices

Primary solids

  • The primary shapes can be extended or rotated to generate volumetric forms or solids that are distinct, regular, and easily recognizable.
    • Circles generate spheres and cylinders
    • Triangles generate cones and pyramids; squares generate cubes.
    • Rectangle generate cube
  • Forms can retain their regularity even when transformed dimensionally or by the addition or subtraction of elements

  • Irregular forms are those whose parts are dissimilar in nature are related to one another in an inconsistent manner, generally asymmetrical and more dynamic

  • Since we deal with both solid masses and spatial voids in architecture, regular forms can be contained within irregular forms. In a similar manner, irregular forms can be enclosed by regular forms.

Articulation of form

  • An articulated form clearly reveals the precise nature of its parts and their relationships to each other and to the whole.

Corner

  • Meeting of two planes
  • Can be visually reinforced by introducing a separate and distinct element

  • An opening can be introduced to one side of the corner, to emphasize the plane bypassing the other (weakens the definition of the volume)

  • If neither plane is intended to define the corner, a volume of space is created to replace the corner (deteriorates the volume of the form, reveals the surface as planes in space)

  • Rounding off the corner emphasizes the continuity of the bounding surfaces of a form


Form & Space

  • How space is conveyed through form?
    • Rise base, lower base, overhead ceiling, column, wall, L-wall, U-wall, closed wall
    • The opening of doorways and windows, difference in elevation to allow passage, sight, light will determine how the space feels
      • Always have two light sources, in order to avoid strong contrast.
