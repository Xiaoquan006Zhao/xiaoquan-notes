  • Show degrees of significance
  • Subordination means focusing on one idea (expressed in the main clause) and arranging points of lesser importance around it, in the form of phrases and dependent clauses.

The Loose Sentence

  • A main clause plus a subordinate construction

We must always be wary of conclusions drawn from the ways of the social insects, since their evolutionary track lies so far from ours. - Robert Ardrey


Adding phrases and clauses to either main constructions or to a preceding subordinate one

I found a large hall, obviously a former garage, dimly lit, and packed With cots. - Eric Hoffer


I knew I had found a friend in the woman, who herself was a lonely soul, never having known the love of man or child. - Emma Goldman



  • Colloquial, informal, relaxed (resembles how people speak)
  • Lacks emphasis and easily becomes formless. Its unity derives not so much from a structural principle as from the coherence of thought.



The Periodic Sentence

  • Reverse the pattern of loose structure, beginning with subordinate constructions and putting the main clauses at the end

If there is no future for the black ghetto, the future of all Negroes is diminished. - Stanley Sanders


  • Adverbial clause

Given a moist planet with methane, formaldehyde, ammonia, and some usable minerals, all of which abound, exposed to lightning or ultraviolet radiation at the right temperature, life might start almost anywhere.

- Lewis Thomas


  • Participial phrase
  • Whatever kinds of subordination it uses, the periodic style is emphatic Delaying the principal thought increases its importance
  • More formal and literary than the loose



The Convoluted Sentence

  • The subordinate elements split the main clause from the inside, often intruding
    • between the subject and the verb
    • between verb and object
    • Within the verb phrase

White men, at the bottom of their hearts, know this. - James Baldwin


And once in a spasm of reflex chauvinism, she called Queen Victoria, whom she rather admired, “a goddamned old water dog.” - William Alfred


Establishes strong emphasis in the main clause

Now demons, whatever else they may be, are full of interest.

- Lytton Strachey


  • Stronger than loose or periodic structure:

Now demons are full of interest, whatever else they may be.

Whatever else they may be, demons are full of interest.


Convoluted structure is formal, and it can tax readers’ attention

Even the humble ambition, which I long cherished, of making sketches of those places which interested me, from a defect of eye or of hand was totally ineffectual. - Sir Walter Scott


The life story to be told of any creative worker is therefore by its very nature, by its diversion of purpose and its qualified success, by its grotesque transitions from sublimation to base necessity and its pervasive stress towards flight, a comedy. - H. C. Wells




The Centered Sentence

  • The main clause is more or less in the middle of the sentence, with subordinate elements on either side

Having wanted to walk on the sea like St. Peter he had taken an involuntary bath, losing his mitre and the better part of his reputation.

- Lawrence Durrell


Standing on the summit of the tower that crowned his church, wings up-spread, sword lifted, the devil crawling beneath, and the cock, symbol of eternal vigilance, perched on his mailed foot, /Saint Michael held a place of his own in heaven and on earth/which seems, in the eleventh century, to leave hardly room for the Virgin of the Crypt at Chartres, still less for the Beau Christ of the thirteenth century at Amiens. - Henry Adams


Help to organize ideas with many (~6) subordinates

  • While not as emphatic as periodic or as informal as loose construction, the centered style enables a writer to place those elements more clearly
  • The chance of obscurity is reduced if the main clause can be placed in the middle of the subordinate elements.

Reflect the natural order of the event or the ideas

After ten years’ fighting to little purpose, after the loss of above a hundred thousand men, and a debt remaining of twenty millions, we at length hearkened to the terms of peace, which was concluded with great advantage to the empire and to Holland, but none at all to us, and clogged soon after with the famous treaty of partition. - Jonathan Swift




The Fragment

  • A single word, a phrase, or a dependent clause standing alone as a sentence.
  • Unclear due to part(s) of the sentence are omitted
    • PARTICIPIAL PHRASE: I saw her. Going down the street
    • ADJECTIVAL CLAUSE: Everyone left except John. Who decided to stay
    • ADVERBIAL CLAUSE: It was very late. When the party broke up
    • Verbless Statement: All people, whether they live in the city or the country.

Used skillfully, they are eye-catching, unusual, and emphatic

“Many a man,” said Speer, “has been haunted by the nightmare that one day nations might be dominated by technical means. That nightmare was almost realized in Hitler’s totalitarian system.” Almost, but not quite.

- Aldous Huxley
