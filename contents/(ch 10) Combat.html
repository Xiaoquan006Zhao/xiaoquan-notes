The Feel of Combat

  • Combat should make the player feel like they are empowered, capable of making winning decisions.
  • Combat should, at certain points, make players feel heroically badass.
  • Nail the combat loop: a short repeatable section of play.
    • If you take that 30 seconds of fun and put it into different environments, with different weapons and enemies, this combat loop becomes incredibly effective.

Some aspects (the book didn’t go in depth)

  • Camera
  • Sound
  • Look and feel of a weapon
  • Impact
  • Celebration

  • Timing and pacing

The Pillars of Combat

  • Genre
    • Understand the genre you’re working in:
      • What are the mechanics that must be there and are non-negotiable?
      • What mechanics are commonly used and you will change them?
      • What other common things in the genre will then have to change to support your key changes?
  • Time
    • Real-time or turn-based? (combination of the two? switch states?)
  • Pacing
    • Think of two or three words to describe the pacing of your game. How do you want players to describe it?
    • DOOM, combat is intense. There are rarely breathers to collect your bearings
    • Hitman, combat is methodical and calculated. Even initiating a hit must be well timed, with many different circumstances taken into account.
  • Feel
    • Think of one word each to describe how you want people to feel as they approach and then enter combat.
      • Are they scared, tense, and fearful?
      • Are they coordinated, organized, and hopeful?
    • If you want a player to feel scared, you need time and space to build that scare.
    • If you want players to feel like they are planning, you need to give them stuff to build a plan about and sometimes a place to do that planning.
    • These intentions are critical for the drama of combat.
  • Participants
    • Is it player vs. player (PvP) or player vs. environment (PvE)?
    • Is this single player or multiplayer?
    • Is it team play or solo?
    • Are AI combatants involved or is it humans only?
  • Progression
    • How players get better in your game?
      • Two primary progression paths for this:
        • Player progression
          • Player’s skill gets better over time, even though the game itself remains unchanged.
        • Character progression.
          • Character the player controls gets better over time
          • The possibility space of the game is narrower.
  • Volume
    • Where does combat fit within the game itself?
    • Is it the game or is it only one aspect of the game?

The Constraints of Combat

  • Create the size, scope, and pacing of encounters.
  • Length of Game
    • How long is your game predicted to be?
    • How many enemies do you need there to be to give you this hours of gameplay?
  • Length of Combat and Number of Combatants
    • How long would you expect the average combat to be?
    • Thinking about the number of combatants as well as the time-to-kill for an average combatant helps us shape a desired length of combat or the number of combatants or both.
  • Frequency of Combat
    • How often do you expect combat to happen?
  • Number of Combatants
    • Does the number grow over time?
    • What are the limitations in terms of game performance?
    • Maximum cognitive load on the player?
    • Project hours/budget available for the design team to create content?
  • Number of Hits
    • How many times do you need to hit a creature in order for it to die?

The Variety of Combat

  • One of the most important things you need to balance in a combat in a game is the player’s ability to remember the possibility space you have created. (It’s that which results in mastery)
  • Keeping the interest of players and providing them agency through variety.
  • It’s not just about having ten different enemies.
  • It’s what’s different about those enemies that forces a player to react, to plan, and to play differently.
    • In the first seconds of any first encounter with an enemy, players are absorbing information, making decisions based on their assumptions about that information, and preparing to react.
  • Variety of Visuals
    • What can visual tell a player?
      • Provides an important insight into the prospective behaviors of the enemy.
  • Variety of Numvers
  • Variety of Behaviors
    • Attacks based on range
    • Range preference
    • Abilities
    • Vulnerabilities
    • Solo vs. Squad behavior
    • Enemy stages
    • Location
      • Size, shape, and cover offered in a location.
      • Might be the difference between light and dark or enemies on different angles
  • Variety of Counters
    • Items, covers, movements, Predictability
  • Variety of Players, Player Characters, and AI Characters
    • Multiplayer games
    • Single player games with multiple characters
    • Symmetric & Asymmetric design
    • Different play styles

Time Outside of Encounters

  • How do you design the time to make the next combat more (what you want players to feel)
  • Anticipation(building up to the next combat)
    • Horror genre, long corridor, back-story, environment storytelling…
  • Recovery(release after the previous combat)
    • Mentally reflecting, gameplay-wise recover…

The Illusion of Winnability

  • It must appear to be winnable to all players, the beginner and the expert. Yet, it must never be truly winnable or it will lose its appeal.
    • PAC-MAN, which appears winnable to most players, yet is never quite winnable.

Cleanliness

  • A dirty game intimidates its beginners with an excess of details.
  • A clean game encourages all players to experiment with the game as it appears.

What trips up the player?

  • Failure arises from some flaw in the game or its controls un-winnable situation.
  • Failure arises from his own limitations, but judges that the game expects or requires superhuman performance un-winnable.
  • Failures to be attributable to correctable errors on his own part winnable and plays on in an effort to master the game.

  1. Find a game where combat is part (but not all) of the experience—most

Now, suppose you wanted to create a similar game except with much more emphasis on combat—nearly all of the game is now combat, and the other elements are virtually nonexistent. What would you imagine that game to play like, and what changes would you have to make to the combat systems to compensate and make it work?

  1. For the same game in the previous question, now imagine removing the combat entirely so that the game focuses on its other elements. How would that change the game, and what other changes would you have to make to the other systems to make up for the loss of combat?
  2. Choose a game that has a memorable AI in some way (particularly good or particularly bad). What about the AI stood out in the game, and why?
  3. Find any game with combat. By either looking it up online or from your own experience, about how long does it take to play through the game beginning to end, and how many total enemies are there in the game? Now divide to get the average time it takes for the player to encounter a new enemy in the game.
  4. Repeat the previous question with several similar games and com- pare them. Which games have the most new enemy content and the least? Which games were seen as better (either through sales or critics’ ratings)?
  5. Take any game with combat and describe its pillars (genre, time, pacing, feel, participants, progression type, volume).
  6. Repeat the previous question for a second game in the same genre as the first, and compare the two. How are they alike, how are they different, and how do the differences in combat pillars manifest in a different play experience?

Side-quests

Side-quest 10.1: Roll-and-Move Combat

  • Choose any board game of the “roll-and-move” genre that has no combat system
    • (examples include Monopoly, Chutes & Ladders, The Game of Life, Clue, or Trivial Pursuit).
  • Design a set of “house rules” that add a PvP combat system to the game. Things you’ll need to specify include
    • What kind of “feel” do you want the combat to have?
      • Give two or three words to describe the combat and/or the feelings you want the player to have from engaging in combat.
    • When does combat initiate—every turn, when landing on or passing over another player, when landing on a specific board space, or some- thing else?
    • What are the actual mechanics of combat? How do these mechanics support or enhance the core gameplay?
    • What happens after combat? That is, how is resolution determined, and what happens to each player who was involved?
  • Your goal is to write these rules in a way that is clear for the reader, feels integrated with the core game, and is a natural extension of it, and that the combat has the right kind of drama and choreography to provide the feelings and goals that you were designing for.

Side-quest 10.2: RPG Asset Budget

  • Congratulations, your team has just successfully pitched a 60-hour epic RPG where the player spends about a third of that time is in combat
    • (the rest is split between exploration, narrative, and optimizing their party in equipment sub-screens).
  • You expect a single typical combat to last about 2 minutes (with boss fights being a bit longer).
  • How many distinct enemies should your team expect to design for this game? Justify your answer through math, making any additional assumptions that you need to for other numbers that aren’t provided here.
