  • Precision means that words serve your purpose—that is, that they express exactly what you think or feel or see or hear.

Concreteness and Abstraction

  • Abstract words signify things that cannot be directly perceived
    • honor, generosity, idea, democracy …
  • Concrete words refer to perceptible things
    • a rose, a clap of thunder, the odor of violets …
  • Often it is a matter of degree. Depending on its context the same term may now be used abstractly, now concretely

CONCRETE: On the hall table a single yellow tea rose stood in a blue vase.

LESS CONCRETE: Roses were growing in the garden.

ABSTRACT: The rose family includes many varieties.


Ground abstraction

  • If you deal with ideas, abstraction is inevitable.
  • Ground reader by using images in the form of examples, analogies, similes, or metaphors

Everything about them is trim and dapper; the stylized flourishes of the white gloved guard, for instance, as he waves the flag for the train to start from Sano station, or the precise unfumbling way the conductor, in equally clean white gloves, clips one’s ticket, arms slightly raised, ticket held at the correct angle and correct distance from the body, clipper engaged and operated in a sharp single movement. - Ronald P. Dore


Ground Concrete

  • When you describe what you see and hear, touch and taste, use the most specific, concrete words you know

TOO ABSTRACT: The large coves are surrounded by various buildings.

MORE CONCRETE: The large coves are surrounded by summer cottages, boat houses, and piers jutting into the water.

EVEN BETTER: The large coves are surrounded by summer cottages, trimly painted, with bright red and blue and green shutters; by boat houses, a few seeming about to slide into the lake, but most still used and well-maintained; and by piers jutting into the water, in good repair with sturdy railings, from which hang clean white life-rings.


Specificity (general v.s. specific)

  • A general word designates a class:
    • Emotion is a general (or generic) term for all kinds of feelings.
  • Fear is more specific, and terror, a particular kind of fear, more specific still.
  • Again it is a matter of degree

Ambiguity

  • Ambiguity means that a word can be read in either of two (more) ways and the context does not make clear which way is intended.

Word having two different senses

It was a funny affair. (“Laughable” or “strange”?)

He’s mad (“Crazy” or “angry”?)


Pronouns

  • may be ambiguous if it is not clear which of two possible antecedents they refer to

Children often anger parents; they won’t talk to them.

We sat near the heater, as /it/was cold. (The “heater” or the unmentioned “room”?)


Connectives

  • Or can signify
    • (1) a logical disjunction, A/or /B but not both
    • (2) an alternative name or word for the same thing:
      • “The shag, or cormorant, is a common sea bird along the New England coast.”
  • Because after a negative statement may also be ambiguous:

We didn’t go because we were tired. (“We did not go and the reason was that we were tired”; or, emphatically, “We did go and we certainly were not tired”?)


Ambiguity lurks in an entire statement

I liked this story as much as I liked all his others. (“I like all his stories, including this one”; or “I don’t like any of his stories, including this one”?)


So be it, until Victory is ours, and there is no enemy, but Peace. (“… there is no enemy, and now we have Peace”; or “ . . . there is no enemy except Peace”?)



Exploit ambiguity as a kind of irony

  • Seeming to say one thing while meaning another

A coronet of seed pearls held her illusion veil. - Joan Didion


  • Using “illusion” both in its technical, dressmaking sense of a bridal veil and in its more commonplace mean- ing of a false hope or dream

Many thanks; I shall lose no time in reading it. - Benjamin Disraeli


  • A standard response to all would-be authors who sent him unsolicited manuscripts

Connotation

  • When a connotation pulls awkwardly against the context, even though the basic meaning of the word fits, the term must be replaced.

In such stories it is exciting to break away from the predictable world we live in and to enter an unrealistic world where anything can happen.


  • The problem is that the writer approves of the story because it stimulates the imagination.
  • But usually unrealistic connotes disapproval
    • (“Don’t be so unrealistic”; “Her plan is too unrealistic to work”).
  • Thus while the basic meaning (or denotation) of unrealistic fits, its connotations do not. Such terms as fantastic, unpredictable, imaginary, wonder-filled would be more appropriate.

Barbarisms

  • A barbarism is either a nonexistent word or an existing one used ungrammatically
    • Inventing new words is not necessarily a fault; imaginative writers create them—neologisms

She’s always been a dutifulled daughter. (For dutiful)


He has great ambitiousness.(For ambition)


Idiom

  • An idiom is a combination of words functioning as a unit of meaning

Colloquial and Pretentious Diction

  • Colloquialisms are expressions appropriate to informal, conversational occasions. In writing they may sound out of place:

We have a swell professor of mathematics.

BETTER: nice, interesting, pleasant


  • An extreme form of colloquialism is slang

Pretentiousness

  • Pretentiousness is using big words to no purpose

Upon receiving an answer in the affirmative, he proceeded to the bulletin board.

BETTER: Told yes, he went to the bulletin board.


  • Skillful writers employ uncommon words to draw attention or to imply a subtlety. Here, for instance, a learned word wittily conceals a vulgar insult:

Among those who distrust the [literary] critic as an intrusive middleman, edging his vast steatopygous bulk between author and audience, it is not uncommon to wish him away, out of the direct line of Vision.

- Carlos Baker


Cliches

  • Cliches are dull and unoriginal.
  • Cliches are verbal molds into which we force experience
  • Instead of shaping reality for ourselves, we accept it, and pass it on,
  • A cliche is a trite expression, one devalued by overuse

an agonizing reappraisal

the bottom line

the finer things of life

the moment of truth

the voice of the people
