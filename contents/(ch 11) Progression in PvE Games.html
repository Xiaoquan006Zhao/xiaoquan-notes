Balance in PvE

  • Advancement, progression, and difficulty in the context of PvE games
    • Single-player games, tabletop RPGs, and multi- player co-op video games

Resistance to Progression

  • Progression requires that the player overcome some form of resistance (friction)
    • Some combination of player skill, luck, and time investment…

Four Phase (The fun of competence)

  • Discovery: the player discovers the existence, nature, and scope of this particular problem. (curiosity, anticipating)
  • Learning: the player invests time and effort in the now-defined problem, interacts with it, pushes at its boundaries, and thinks about it. (experiment)
  • Mastery: the player’s understanding of the problem finally reaches critical mass, and the pieces start falling into place. (aha moment)
  • Fluency: the player ultimately adds the new skill to their skill set, and can now use the skill on other similar problems encountered later. (how far they have come from the start)



Actual vs. Perceived Progression


The Four Elements of Perceived Difficulty

  • Player skill: the level of the player’s actual skill at the game.
  • Virtual skill: the player’s power level in the game.
    • Doubling their health or the amount of damage improves their ability to win.
  • Virtual challenge: Based purely on power level of enemies or challenges in the game: increasing stats or adding enemies
  • Skill challenge: Player must develop new and novel ways to play in order to succeed.
    • Enemies that have more sophisticated AI that make them more challenging to defeat
    • Introducing new types of challenges, or deep puzzles that require multiple skills used together.


Progression Arcs and Loops

  • Progression arc: one-time progression from one point to another
  • Progression loop: repeating cycle of progression
    • The current iteration in the current loop
      • What player is doing right now?
    • Foresight in the current loop
      • Player’s understanding of the future outcome of progressing
    • Near-future loops
      • The progression loop(s) that become available once the player progress
    • Far-future loops
      • A series of additional steps the player must do (They don’t foresight)
    • Anchoring goals
      • The ultimate long-term goals that the player is aware of and desires. (Base motivation to go through all of these individual iteration through the loops)
    • Exits
      • Ways of the players to leave the current loop, to do something else.

Cost

  • Engaging with a progression loop has a cost
    • Direct costs: cognitive load and time spent
    • Resource costs
    • Opportunity costs
    • Sunk costs of previous interactions


  • In Metroidvania games, the player is motivated by a desire to gain access to new areas and new skills and abilities which leads to a desire to master the most recently acquired skill or explore the most recently discovered area. The loop is repeated.
  • In narrative-driven games (such as Her Story or Life Is Strange), the player is motivated by seeing the story develop and resolve.
    • On successive iterations of the narrative progression loop, the player gains a greater understanding of the plot and world and backstory, and relatedness to the characters.

Velocity and Acceleration

  • The higher the resistance, the slower the player’s progress

  • Velocityof progression: what is the change in a player’s progression over time
  • Acceleration: the rate of change in velocity.
    • Even if a player is progressing slowly, if they detect that their rate of progress is increasing so that they move forward faster if they just keep going a little longer, that can be enough to make them not feel so demoralized at their perceived lack of forward movement.

Regression and Loss

  • Progression isn’t always forward; a player can lose progress through failure or error.
  • The fear of losing hard-earned progress—loss aversion—can be extremely emotionally impactful and is also related to the concept of overall perceived difficulty of a game.
    • Motivator for caution, flee, rage-quit…

D&D

  • In early editions of Dungeons & Dragons, some monsters (particularly higher-level undead) had an attack that could literally drain experience levels from a player’s character. (lost several weeks to months of progress, the fear is over death because there are resurrection spells)
    • While many players absolutely hated the level-drain mechanic for obvious reasons, it was highly effective at giving players the same healthy fear of the undead that their characters should have.

Potential loss and & Probability of loss


Juiciness

  • The type and intensity of sensory feedback used to inform the player of their progress.
    • Adding juiciness to a game at the moment of progression
      • (such as the player leveling up, finding a treasure chest, or entering a new zone) can increase the player’s perception of how much they just gained, especially in relation to the juiciness of other lesser gains.

Types of Progression


  • Individual progression arcs
    • Accomplishment, Discovery…
  • Individual progression loops
    • Difficulty, complexity, power, cosmetic content…
  • Social progression
    • Influence, ranking, guilds…

Motivations for Progression

  • Intrinsic(motivated by the player’s own internal drives and desires)
  • Extrinsic(motivated by player’s desire to be given an external reward, or to avoid an external punishment).

Intrinsic is powerful, but not over-riding

  • If a player is performing an activity due to an intrinsic motivation to do so, and then an extrinsic motivator is added and then later removed, the intrinsic motivation is extinguished and the original behavior stops.
  • Designers should therefore be cautious about rewarding players for “good” behavior or punishing them for “bad” behavior

Some example of intrinsic motivators and progression and rewards

Agency: control over one’s environment and one’s own fate
  • Motivates: seek improved character
  • Reward: Virtual skill
    • Stat boosts, new weapons or capabilities
  • Typical progression: player skill increase within a level and meet a spike in difficulty (boss), then later gaining more improvement


Mastery: greater understanding over how to manipulate a system
  • Motivates: increase the difficulty or complexity of the game
  • Reward: increased player skill
  • Typical Progression: amount and quality of tutorials, skill gating
    • Rogue-like: the only thing improved is the player knowledge and skill, since everything resets each run.

Curiosity: finding out what happens next
  • Motivates: enter a discovery arc
  • Reward: additional knowledge of the game
    • Exploration of virtual spaces and advancing the narrative.
    • Progression through level transitions (that is, when the player enters a new level or area) is a special kind of reward, because it shows at a visceral level that the player is moving ahead.
    • Story itself has a “difficulty” or “friction” of sorts (dramatic tension),
      • Whether the dramatic tension of the story overlaps with the perceived difficulty curves of the game.
      • Bad example


Closure: finishing what one started
  • Motivates: enter an accomplishment arc
  • Reward: satisfaction of completion
  • What drives some players to completionism?
    • The field of psychology describes the Zeigarnik effect, which states that it is easier for people to remember incomplete tasks than completed ones.
    • Players feel a sense of persistent cognitive load as long as they leave a task unfinished, and they experience a sense of relief when completing a long-standing goal.
  • Example
    • Sid Meier’s Civilization, “just one more turn”
      • The active progression loops are constantly open and ongoing. Once the current loop completes, the next opens
      • Progression loops overlap, the player is never more than a turn or two away from completing some goals

Social Progression

  • Require interaction with other players to have meaning
    • Player could find ways to enable it if the game does not support
    • But it can be explicitly design

Expression: a show of one’s individuality and uniqueness
  • Motivates: collect cosmetic content or make character-defining choices
  • Reward: self-expression

Connection: relatedness to and fellowship with other humans
  • Motivates: enter guilds
  • Reward: feeling of social inclusion

Superiority: showing domination of one’s peers
  • Motivates: climb leaderboards and other ranking systems
  • Reward: Thrill and recognition of competing and winning

Community: showing value to one’s fellow players
  • Motivates: enter an influence loop, performing behaviors that win friends
  • Reward: Friends, recognition of the player’s value

Sociopathy
  • Motivates: Trolling/griefing, break social taboos, rush of power that comes from causing pain to others
  • Reward: Negative attention

False Progression

  • Slot machines, when a player has a “near miss” where it appears they are just one reel away from a jackpot
    • This can make the player feel as if they’re getting closer to hitting a true jackpot
  • Destiny, On initial release, most loot drops did not give players anything better than what they already had. But some players feel they need to get enough random loot before getting the next useful one. (they falsely feel they are closer to the goal)
    • Could have trading or recycling to mitigate the problem, but does not solve it.
    • Exchange rate: how many unwanted items (or proportionally, how much play time spent grinding to get those items) does it take to get one item that the player does want?

Reward Schedules

  • You want the player to feel like they earned their progression and that they are being rewarded for playing well.
    • Player needs to notice that they are being rewarded (Usually not a problem, but a certain amount of flourishes that call attention can be nice)
  • Giving too many rewards too quickly diminishes their impact
  • Giving too few rewards, the player become demoralized and feel like they are playing the game wrong.

Random or intermittent reward schedule

  • A random or intermittent reward schedule has more impact than a fixed schedule.
    • The rewards can’t be arbitrary, still linked to the player’s progress and as a direct result of actions
  • Some bad examples:
    • Sign-in rewards (arbitrary), waters down the player’s hard-earned achievements
    • Hidden achievements, suppose to be a reward for skill, but player can’t demonstrate that skill if the goal is unknown
    • Luck-based achievements, (winning gambling 5 times in a row), no direct control
      • Ask yourself what the player is going to feel rewarded for, exactly.
  • Some good examples:
    • Random loot drops in typical RPG or action games
    • Crushing damage cut scene In MK11
    • Sid Meier’s Civilization, concurrent progression loops, staggered to give small rewards every few turns, more loops open unpredictable

Layer

  • Caution: When major reward is happening, don’t distract player with minor rewards


Death

  • Failing but also progressing
  • Rogue-likes (FTL, Desktop Dungeons, Dungeon of the Endless)
    • Unlock new gameplay elements when the player wins or dies

Common Problems in PvE

Differing Player Skill Levels

  • The typical solution is to implement a series of difficulty levels, with higher levels granting the AI power-based bonuses or giving the player a power dis- advantage
    • Because that is relatively cheap and easy to design and implement.
  • To set and manage player expectations up front, especially about higher difficulties and especially if the game actually cheats
  • Dynamic Difficulty Adjustment(DDA), a special type of negative feedback loop where the game tries to figure out how the player is doing and then adjusts the difficulty level during play based on how the player is doing.
    • Caution: DDA punishes players for doing well
      • If it is player skill, then being punished feels bad
      • If it is an exploit, then it should be fine
    • Exploit the DDA, playing poorly in the beginning then rush to victory
    • A variance: Let players choose
      • Detecting if they are dong well or bad, offer them a chance to switch difficulty
        • In God of War, if the player dies enough times on a level, the game offers the player the chance to change the difficulty level when on the game over/reload screen.
      • Offer them more challenging or safer content simultaneously
        • Mario Kart — short cuts
        • flOw — player can go to the next (more challenging) level or the previous (easier) level at just about any time.

The Commander Problem

  • One player can dominate the entire game, telling other players what to do and generally turning the experience into a solitaire game
    • Common in many earlier tabletop cooperative games, such as Pandemic and Flash Point: Fire Rescue
    • Usually, in information transparent games with plenty planning time
      • Possible solution:
        • Privileged information
          • Hanabi
        • Real-time mechanics
        • Restricted Communication
        • One against many
          • Scotland Yard, Descent: Journeys in the Dark
          • Sharing information might be beneficial to the enemy
  • The extent to which this is a true problem depends on the interpersonal dynamics and personalities of the players involved.

Discussion Questions

  2. What are some ways a game designer can modify their game to change

the difficulty as perceived by the player?

  1. What is the difference between a gameplay arc and a gameplay loop?
  2. Choose any game that features permadeath as a mechanic. Suppose you

changed the game so that when the player died, they simply respawned in a safe place a few feet away with no further penalty. How do you think this would change the play experience—would players play the game differently, would it appeal to a different kind of player, and how would the “feel” of the game change overall?

  1. Of the motivations listed in this chapter (agency, mastery, curiosity, closure, expression, connection, superiority, community), which of these most motivates you when you play?
  2. What are some ways to keep the player feeling challenged in a game as their skill increases?
  3. Choose any co-op game that you’re familiar with. What, if anything, does the game do about the Commander Problem? Is it effective?
  4. Choose any game that keeps going after a player reaches the end, into an elder game state. What does the game do, if anything, to maintain player interest in the elder game?
  5. What is a core loop? Why is it important?
  6. Choose any game that has a reputation for its difficulty level (either very

high or very low). Search for the marketing materials for this game, such as trailer videos or advertisements. Based on this marketing, who do you think the target audience was intended to be? Does the difficulty make sense based on that audience?


Side-quests

Side-quest 11.1: Removal of Level Caps

  • Choose any game that has a “level cap” or some other hard limitation on one or more forms of player progression, leading into an elder game.
  • Think for a moment about why that cap exists at all.
  • Suppose for a moment that the cap was removed, and players could continue progressing indefinitely.
    • How would this affect the game?
    • What problems or issues with the existing game would be fixed?
    • What new problems or issues would be created?

Side-quest 11.2: Reward Schedules

  • Choose any game that has one or more progression arcs or loops in it, preferably a game you have played a lot and are very familiar with.
  • Looking at the game’s mechanics and content, reverse-engineer the reward schedule for the game. Analyze it as follows:
    • What are the different kinds of progression in the game and the rewards for each type?
    • For each kind of progression, how often do rewards happen?
      • Is it on a fixed or random schedule (or does it seem random on the first playthrough, but it’s actually fixed if the player is already familiar with the game)?
      • Does the schedule follow an obvious pattern, or is it irregular?
      • Do certain types of progression (or rewards) get emphasized over others, either at certain times in the game or overall?
      • Consider the combined reward schedule of all progression loops overall. Specifically:
        • How often does the player receive a reward?
        • What’s the approximate mean time between rewards?
        • Are there any points in the game where the player receives a larger number of rewards at once, where all or most of the progression loops finish an iteration at the same time?
        • How does the game feel at that point—is there a strong sense of closure? How often do these points occur?
        • Are there any long stretches in the game where the player receives few or no rewards?
        • How do those sections of the game feel when playing, relative to other sections of the game?
        • Do they stand out in some way?

Side-quest 11.3: Modeling Rewards

  • Choose any game with a progression or loot system or similar (such as a computer RPG).
  • Model that system over the course of the game, either in a spreadsheet or in a visualization tool like Machinations
