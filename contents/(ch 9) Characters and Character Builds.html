Character Creation Systems

  • Character-based mechanics can give players
    • Meaningful and interesting choices
    • Support multiple play styles
    • Improve replay value of a game.

Character Selection

  • Common in games that have a relatively short play time (MOBA, Fighting/Brawling games, Racing games, co-op board games)
  • Also in longer-form games like RPG, to support quick access instead of mucking about in attribute screens for half a day

Attributes (Stats)

  • For games where player is expected to use their character in many different ways to influence the game world, the character needs to be defined in terms of exactly what they can do.
    • In the original D&D, characters had six attributes (Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma)

Skills

  • Either enable a player to take a new action in the game that they couldn’t without the skills (active), or provide the player an additional bonus that is in play all the time (passive)
    • Characters may not have a particular skill, but all character share the same set of attributes

Equipment

  • RPG, various weapons, armors, magic items…
  • FPS, main weapon, sidearm, gears or grenades…

Character Classes

  • In some games, your character is the sum of your attributes and/or skills
    • It’s entirely chosen by you not assigned by the game
  • In other games, characters are organized by class (pushes a direction of certain play style(s) more)
    • Class-specific skills
      • Their can backstab, fighters can not

Levels of Viability

  • Build: Combination all terms mentioned above

How balanced is a build?

  • Optimal (min-maxed),
  • Viable,
  • Marginally viable,
  • Nonviable,
    • If a build in nonviable, the quicker a player realizes that, the better
    • 30 Hours in to find a build to be nonviable, most player will stop playing
    • Unless they are aware of what they doing, like a magician who puts all points into strength (They expect it has a higher chance of not working compare to other builds)

Balancing for Character Progression

  • As the games progresses, the power gap between optimal and viable build tends to be bigger.
    • A typical player will be at 85–110, and a min-max player can pump it to 300–600, there will be some people complaining about the balance of your game.
  • How does a game balance itself so that it is not too easy for optimal builds and not too hard for viable builds?
    • If the game is marketed towards a more casual (or hardcore) players, the balance can toward one extreme or the other
    • Easy, normal, hard Mode (not good I think)
    • (Dynamic difficult by game and by player choice)

God Stats, Dump Stats, and Meaningful Decisions

  • Character creation is about providing the player meaningful decisions.
    • A decision that has some impact on the game and that offers a choice between several viable alternatives.
    • Some example of not meaningful decisions
      • Two swords with identical stats but one cost cheaper. Get the cheaper one (assuming not trying to show off)
      • A blind choice where player has no way of evaluating
      • A choice that end up giving the same result
        • Some choices may have emotional value, but they should all have some impact gameplay-wise
  • God-stat: a stat that is overpowered or, at the very least, necessary for an optimal build.
  • dump stat: something that a player can safely ignore, because it simply isn’t required at all.
    • Non-spell characters will not care about a stat that only increase the damage of a spell
  • If a player fails to maximize a god stat or puts otherwise perfectly good attribute points into a dump stat, all they’ve managed to do is weaken their character.
    • There may be good reason behind those decisions (backstories, inside jokes…)
  • To avoid a build being too weak and players being punished to a point the game is unplayable, a designer attempts to make all elements of a character build at least marginally useful to all characters.
    • A stereotypical magic user class may not need Strength, but if Strength also affects carrying capacity (how much loot you can carry) and ability to push obstacles like heavy doors (accessing treasure rooms or other contents), even the magic-using characters will get some use out of Strength (They make meaningful choice when considering Strength over other stats)

Play-testing notes

  • if you notice that there’s a particular attribute or skill that all of your play-testers seem to take (or avoid), that suggests an imbalance to be addressed.
  • If characters in play-testing that seem viable early game and become nonviable later on, or else testers that are intentionally trying to make good characters that make nonviable ones anyway.
    • This suggests that the player is making blind choice without having the information to understand what their character build will do or what challenges they’ll face.
    • To avoid this,
      • Modify the game’s content so these characters become playable
      • Modify the character attribute/skill system so that the characters are viable
      • Find ways to prevent a player from making a nonviable build
        • Making certain essential skills “standard”
        • (Progressively introduce what is required (and ramp up the challenge) so the players have a chance to gradually shift their build on the viable path (they meet obstacle, they find ways to overcome it) instead of meet a giant wall) (A better approach in my opinion)
  • if you’re finding that just about every character is viable but they all play exactly the same, the same general strategies work regardless of build. This suggest the choice may not matter gameplay-wise.

Balance and Player Perception

  • The god-stats and dump-stats may be legitimate which suggest a change in the mathematical or design system.
  • Or they may just be player perception which suggest a change in how the game communicates the value to players
    • Assumptions based on prior game experience
      • If you violate this, it may need extra steps to make sure player realizes it
    • Lack of feedback from the game interface
      • If the player is currently leveling up and can +1 to an attribute of their choice, how are the effects of this choice made visible to the player?
      • Perhaps a way to show them the effects (secondary attributes), the skills that are affected. (Players might choose differently if given the extra information)
    • Confusing or overwhelming feedback from the game interface
      • Long text or hard to conceptualize math operations (+13% when you damage range is 65-78, most people will not do this)
      • If the game gives complete information, it may cause decision paralysis. They stop thinking and just make a decision.
    • How the data is presented (referred more to loss aversion and other aspects of decision making)
      • A strength stat in the range of 1-9
        • Player has a base attack damage of 1 and gains +1 for every point of Strength
        • Player has a base attack damage of 6 and gains +1 for every point of Strength above 5, -1 for every point of Strength below 5
        • Exactly the same mathematically, players will tend to have a stronger aversion to an Attack below 5 since that is presented to them as a penalty

Discussion Questions

  1. What is a character build?
  2. Choose any game with some kind of character creation or character

build system. What are some of the most popular character builds? (If you’re unfamiliar with the game, you might look this up in online forums or strategy guides.)

  1. For the same game as in the previous question, what are the different play styles that the game supports? (Looking at the different character builds may give you some clues here—each popular build probably fits neatly into one particular play style.)
  2. If a particular character build is found to be overpowered, what are some possible root causes that a designer should look for?
  3. Think of a game that allows a great deal of character customization. Is it possible to make a completely nonviable build in this game, and if so, give an example.
  4. In games with a point-buy stat system, why is the presence of a ‘god stat’ or ‘dump stat’ a potential sign of imbalance?
  5. What is your favorite character build, in any game? Why?
  6. Think of any skill or any single piece of equipment in a game you’re familiar with. What kinds of play styles or character builds is this item optimal for, and which ones would it never be used with?
  7. Which would be worse: shipping a PvP game with ten character classes where one of them is entirely nonviable, or where one of them is extremely overpowered?
  8. Choose a single-player game that involves some kind of progression, where the player makes choices about how to level their character (either through point-buy stat gains on leveling up, finding new equipment in their loadout, unlocking new skills in a skill tree, or similar choices). How forgiving is this system to suboptimal choices—that is, how much does a player have to optimize in order to beat the game, or how suboptimal can a character build be while still having the game be theoretically beatable?

Side-quests

Side-quest 9.1: Three Attributes, Three Builds

  • Suppose you’re designing a multiplayer RPG
    • (this could be either a tabletop game, an MMO, or a couch-coop experience, or any other situation you might imagine).
  • You’re designing such that each player controls a single character, and the players work together in a small group to overcome the game’s challenges.
  • Three general roles:
    • A “DPS” role whose primary function is to do large amounts of damage to enemies
    • A “Tank” role who draws enemy attacks away from the DPS character so they don’t die
    • A “Support” role whose job is to keep the Tanks alive and/or buff the damage output of the DPS characters in order to increase the overall effectiveness of the team.
  • Design an attribute system for this game where all characters have exactly three attributes. You may name these attributes whatever you want.
  • Design the attributes such that all three attributes have at least some value to all three roles. You do not have to go into great detail describing exactly how each attribute should work; rather, give a general description of what kind of effect the attributes have.
  • As an example of the level of complexity to go for here, you might design an attribute called Compassion which you describe as a stat that increases all buff and de-buff effects that the character creates. (This would be a terrible design for an attribute since it would be a god stat for Support characters and a dump stat for both DPS and Tanks.)

Side-quest 9.2: Classes for Players

  • Game designer Richard Bartle wrote a widely read essay on four types of players that commonly play MUDs (games that were the precursors to today’s MMOs).
    • “Achievers”: players who want to “win” the game (even though there isn’t technically a win state). These are the players who want to create optimal builds, collect all of the rarest loot, get to the top of the rankings in PvP arenas, and generally just create the most overpowered character possible.
    • “Explorers”: players who want to see everything the game has to show them. These are the players who would gleefully draw maps of the world if there were no auto-mapping feature and who would experiment with the physics, mechanics, and other elements of the game just to see how they work.
    • “Socializers”: players who are there for the company of other human players. These people prefer to spend more time talking and interacting with their fellow players, sometimes casually, sometimes role-playing in character, and might be more concerned with how their character looks than what kinds of stats they have.
    • “Killers”: more commonly called “griefers” today, these are the players who find fun in ruining the experience of other players. This may take the form of attacking other players and looting their bodies, and/or verbally abusing them in chat.
  • Choose any MMO, past or present, that has the concept of distinct character classes. If you are not familiar with the game, look up a strategy guide or wiki online that lists the classes available.
  • Choose any three character classes in the game (try to go for a variety), and read about the general role that class plays in the game, general play style, and what strategies players can do to optimize their builds.
  • For each of the classes you chose, write a single paragraph noting whether you think that class was created specifically to appeal to one or more of Bartle’s four player types listed above.
    • If so, mention why: what is it about the strategy or play style that would appeal to which types?
    • If not, what kind of player (not listed above) do you think it would appeal to, if any?
