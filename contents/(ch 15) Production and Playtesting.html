Early Development

  • You want to get the “core” of the game—the moment-to-moment gameplay that’s repeated throughout—to be solid.
  • Make the game fun first, worry about balance later.

How do you assign data with no playtest data or analytics?

  • Make everything OP or make nothing OP
    • Don’t make most things are mundane and a few things are totally OP


  • Think about the overall strengths and characteristics that define the thing you’re designing.
    • Once you know what this thing is supposed to do, figure out what numbers will get it there.
  • Create the bare minimum amount of content you can in order to have a game that can be playtested and that will showcase the main mechanics, strategies, and play styles that you intend to support.
    • For a MOBA, you don’t need 100+ characters
  • Document your design purpose and design knobs
    • What number can be changed?
    • Design constrains…

First Playable

  • Testing to see if the game is engaging, if the core mechanics work
  • Pay attention to the macro-level between broad strategies, character builds, or play styles

Feedback

  • “I don’t like this particular thing about the game,”
    • Useful information; they’ve alerted you to a problem. Your next step is to figure out why they don’t like it, then fix the root cause.
  • “I think you should change this part of the game to this other thing,”
    • Less useful
    • First, you have to probe to figure out what element of the game the tester isn’t having fun with
    • Then, do the same as the first case

Player criticism

  • Player criticisms of your game come down to this:
    • The ideal game they’re envisioning in their head is different from the one they’re playing.
  • Your goal, as the designer, is to first uncover what game they’re thinking about
  • Then deciding whether that is in fact the game you’re trying to build
  • Then changing either your game’s presentation (to make it clear what kind of game you’re actually making) or the game itself.

Pre-Alpha

  • Start setting up some spreadsheets with formulas to show the relationships between various mechanics
  • If you plan on using metrics and analytics to assist with game balance later on
    • Design your analytics hooks in the game

Alpha

  • All of the mechanics are in the game, the basic engine is functional, and you can pretty much play with all the systems
  • Content grind, filling out all of the monster and item tables, character roster, dialogue trees, level layouts, and everything else that goes in the game.

Not enough time to playtest all match ups in Fighting games, MOBA, TGC…

  • If you created one or more “vanilla” assets in earlier phases to test the core mechanics with, this is where you’ll balance things against those baselines.

Mathematical balance starts to pick up

  • If you don’t know how to balance, try extremes and drastic changes to gain some insights about feeling of numbers

Playtesting

  • Still with major focus on finding exploits
  • Gain a more accurate feeling about the model
    • Do players agree with what you want them to experience

Some play-tester types

  • Play to win: Finding exploit
  • Angry: Hyper-sensitive about disturbing flow
  • Style: Always plays the same way (sniper always plays long-range guns)
    • See if a particular strategy that you want to be viable actually is
  • Newbie: How other new player might approach your game
    • People who don’t play games or your genre often
  • Griever: Have the most fun when they ruin someone else’s fun
    • Knowing what a player can do to ruin other’s experience can set appropriate boundaries later
  • Pro-gamer: Rely on their inherent skill, hate losing to randomness or luck

Beta

  • The entire game can be played end-to-end in its entirety.
  • A lot of balancing work happens
  • Open or Closed-Beta
    • Sufficiently large data sets to use analytics
  • Perception over actual fairness
    • Balance the things that players see and perceive directly
      • A weapon with random hit range 0 to +10 or -3 to +15 won’t be clearly felt
      • Changing the physics engine will be obvious

Post-Launch

  • Balance patches and new content
  • For games that have continued development after release
  • Same process (Alpha-Beta…) for new content but on shorter time-lines

Playtesting for Balance

  • Playtesting with a purpose
    • For the design, what are you trying to find
      • If there are exploits in the game
    • For the tester, what should you focus
      • Instruct them to play for win

Discussion Questions

  1. What happens if you start balancing the game too early in development?
  2. What happens if you wait too long and start balancing the game too

late in development?

  1. When in development should you start with small-scale playtesting

with tests observed by the designer directly?

  1. What is the danger of creating a mathematical model of your game in a

spreadsheet at the very beginning, as you’re first designing the mechanics and before the game is playable?

  1. What is the difference between “feature complete” and “content complete”?
  2. What is the difference between how a game designer might observe

the results of playtests in a closed-beta environment and an open-beta
environment?

  1. At what point in development would you be designing the analytics

that a game will be collecting? At what point will you be making the
most use of the analytics data collected?

  1. Of the six kinds of playtesters listed at the end of the Alpha section of this

chapter

  • Which of those are you most like when you playtest?
  • Which of those personas would be hardest for you to emulate, if you were asked to?
  1. Suppose you’re running a playtest of a Metroidvania game where the main character starts with a short-range gun as their primary attack, and a tester tells you that they think you should increase the range.
  • How do you respond?
  1. What are some reasons you might playtest a game other than testing for

balance?


Side-quests

Side-quest 15.1: Cheat Codes

  • Choose any video game that has some kind of secret cheat code system
  • For the game you selected, research all of the available cheats and put them in a list.
  • For each cheat, state how you think it might have been used by the development team during testing.

Side-quest 15.2 : BattleBattle

  • http://stonetronix.com/gdc-2013/battlebattle.pdf

  • The rules are listed on the last page.
  • This is a two-player game that requires 6d6 (or 2d6 and some other stackable tokens such as small coins or beads to keep track of health and special ability uses remaining)
  • First, familiarize yourself with the game mechanics. Near the end is a page with four copies of one card, called “Vanilla.”
    • This card starts with 5 health and 3 tokens which can each be used to add 1 to your battle die.
  • Start by playing a game of Vanilla against Vanilla
  • Once you’re comfortable playing the basic rules of BattleBattle, it’s time to introduce the 28 characters that were designed as part of this game on the first seven pages.
  • If playing on your own, choose a character at random
  • if playing in a group, distribute characters to each pair of players, one character per pair.
  • Most of these characters are not balanced.
    • Rather than deal with the 756 possible pairings, test them against Vanilla instead.
    • Play the character selected for you against Vanilla, for long enough to determine if the character is too weak, too strong, or just about right.
  • You may then make one or two changes to the character
    • Modify their health or number of tokens, one of their special abilities, or one of the rolls of a battle die.
      • This game has many “design knobs” to influence the power of a character. After making the change, put that character back in the rotation, choose a different character to play against Vanilla, and continue.

Consider for each character

  • Is the character fun to play? Does it allow for player agency and interesting choices?
    • Negative example: Dalekin the original set of characters has no choices at all
  • Is the character balanced against Vanilla? Is it unlikely that many other characters will have strong (or weak) matchups against this one?
    • Negative example: Catin the original set of characters works very differently from most others, in that it uses its tokens to avoid damage instead of its health. Mechanism to interact with token will make Cat too strong or too weak even if Cat is balanced with Vanilla.
  • Do the character’s mechanics match its theme? Does the name of the card suggest what it does in a way that feels natural and seamless?
    • Negative example: Sniperin the original set has no special abilities that make it feel particularly sniper-like.


  • Play tournament and keep track of win-rate
    • Is the game a blowout or close
